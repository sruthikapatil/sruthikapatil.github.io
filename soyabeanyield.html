Soybean Yield Factors

Several factors influence soybean yields, including:

  • Water availability: Under rainfed conditions, good soybean yields are between 1.5 and 2.5 tons per hectare (ton/ha) of seed.

  • Irrigation: High yields of improved varieties are between 2.5 and 3.5 ton/ha under irrigation.

  • Fertilization: The content of zinc, copper, phosphorus, sulfur, and potassium in the soil can affect soybean yield.

  • Planting date: The date of planting controls solar radiation capture and water availability, which are the main predictors of soybean yield.

  • Soil depth: The A horizon depth and total soil depth can affect soybean yield.

Note: The global average soybean yield has increased from around 1,128 to around 2,769 kg/ha over the past 60 years.

The average soybean yield per acre in the United States from 2018 to 2022 was 47.0 bushels per acre. The yield varies by farm, with the average yield for a typical Canadian farm being 34.4 bushels per acre and the average yield for a typical Indiana farm being 58.3 bushels per acre.

The USDA projects that the 2024 U.S. soybean yield will be 53.2 bushels per acre, based on 86.3 million harvested acres.

Soybean yield is influenced by the following factors:

  • Genetics: Improvements in genetics have contributed to the increase in soybean yields in the U.S. over the past 50 years.

  • Management: Management practices can also contribute to increased yields. For example, applying herbicides before the soybeans emerge can prevent weeds from stealing nutrients, light, and moisture from the crop.

  • Number of nodes: The number of nodes a soybean plant develops is closely linked to its yield potential. Nodes are the parts of the plant where leaves are attached to the stem.

  • Variety: Different varieties of soybeans have varying maturity periods and different levels of resistance to pests, diseases, and insects. Farmers can grow multiple varieties with different maturity periods to reduce the risk of yield reduction due to climate or biotic factors.

Source: USDA & Agricultural Research Organizations
